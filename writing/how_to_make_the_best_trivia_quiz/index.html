How to make the best trivia quiz

Written by: Matthew

Originally posted: 18th of November, 2025
Last updated: 14th of March, 2026

Introduction

If you look up "trivia questions" on ye olde Google search, you will find SEO optimised garbage talking about trivia in the context of team building exercises. You know, the ones that bureaucracy at large faceless corporations push managers to do once in a while.

For the past couple of years however, I have been consistently making and hosting trivia quizzes every fortnight. Not because someone told me to, but because I really do love to do it.

Just for you, here is all the knowledge I have learnt in hopes that you will find it useful.

How do you choose the right question?

A good trivia question is one that touches just the tip of the person's knowledge iceberg in their mind. The participants should feel like if they tried hard enough, they could reach into the catacombes of the back of their minds, filled with spider webs and funky smells, to find the answer.

Unfortunately, you might realise that this goal is pretty unrealistic. Everyone has a different model of how the world works, and we all interact with it in different ways. Therefore, no question will have such an effect on every single participant in the room.

Since we can't achieve perfection, we will instead optimise for "good enough". This is where you choose questions that you (as the quizmaster) like. If you find a question makes you groan or facepalm, then move on. If a question catches your eye, then consider adding it to the quiz. Remember to stay true to yourself, don't think about it from the perspective of your participants because you are not a participant yourself.

You're the host, remember?

Other notes on question selection

Try to include a wide variety of questions spanning different domains. Here is a non-exhaustive list of topics I have used in the past:

  • Geography
  • Mathematics
  • Food/cooking
  • Sciences
    • Biology
    • Chemistry
    • Physics
  • Historical
    • Paintings
    • Dates of specific events
  • What does __ stand for?
  • Astronomy
  • Translating phrases or words
  • Sports

Keep trick questions at a minimum. People feel unfairly cheated when they realise their mistakes during the marking phase of the trivia session. It is kinda funny though to see close friends/friendgroups participating in the quiz to berate each other on their silly mistakes, which is why I don't recommend you to remove them completely. Let people have some fun that way.

Try to avoid questions that are politically loaded. People choose to participate in trivia quizzes because they want to forget about the outside world for a little while. Therefore, such questions do more harm than good to the enjoyment of your quiz. Questions such as "When did World War 2 end?" are probably fine. Historical, not political.

Images

An image's purpose in a trivia quiz is to help the participants parse the question. If a question is worded clunkly, the image will save it. If a question is too long or complex to word in a concise manner, the image will save it. Once you understand this, an image can come in really handy.

Pick high resolution images wherever possible, a pixelated image will stick out like a sore thumb and make it seem unprofessional.

Clipart/cartoon style images should be used in place of the actual subject matter of the question when it is unappealing to look at. If you have a question like "How many hearts does an octopus have?", using an image of a heart extracted from an actual octopus might make some people feel sick.

Make sure you don't include the answer in the image itself. It might sound obvious, however it happened to me. The question was something along the lines of "In what year did Usain Bolt set the world record time in the 100m sprint". The image I included of him had the answer right on his chest (whoops). Double check your work before you send it off.

If the question is easy enough to parse to not necessarily require an image, putting a funny picture that is related to the subject is a good idea.

Tiebreaker questions

The best tiebreaker questions should rely on esoteric, numerical based knowledge. For example, "How many stairs are on the Eiffel Tower?" The last thing you want during the tiebreaker round is for there to be yet another tie. It is not a good look on you as the quizmaster, even though others might find it funny. Keep a calculator handy, and learn how to find the difference between two numbers (hint: subtraction)

Miscellaneous

Check that your answer sheet matches with the order in which you have said out loud.

LLMs like ChatGPT are great tools, but they churn out the same questions if you prompt them the same way everytime. They might also output out the wrong answer, so double check their answer via Google before use.

If you plan for your trivia quizzes to be a regular occurrence, keeping a similar template/presentation style of your slideshows is a good idea. People like consistency and routine, and seeing that same coloured background on your slideshow will keep nerves down and make people feel more at home.

Try to show up as early as you can. If a newcomer goes to the venue where you host your trivia quizzes and see that it is empty and/or dark, they won't wait around for another five minutes. They will instead leave and never return again.

A ten question quiz roughly takes 15 to 20 minutes to host from start to finish.

If you are writing answers on paper, ensure that they go in the recycling bin at the end. If there isn't one in the venue, organise one beforehand. Keep spare pens/pencils handy for when someone inevitably forgets to bring one.

By the end of my quiz making journey, it took me roughly one to two hours to create one from start to finish.

Afterword

At least one piece of trivia making advice that I have given out so far is bad, I can guarantee that. Therefore, it is up to you to make up your own mind on the best practices when it comes to making the best trivia quiz. Trust your own intuition, and rapidly test and trial new ways in order to improve the experience of participants.

Because trivia can be fun!
