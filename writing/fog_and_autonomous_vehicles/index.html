Fog and autonomous vehicles

Written by: Matthew

Originally posted: 28th of June, 2024
Last updated: 30th of June, 2025

Observation

On the bus drive this morning, we encountered a suffocating amount of fog.

For most of the ride, you couldn't see for more than a few hundred meter out in front. The most visible element of the road were the centre yellow lines, as compared to the white lines of the shoulder of the road.

Thankfully, other vehicles were easy to spot due to the advent of fog light. I noted that I could see cars painted any black more clearly as compared to white cars. I soon came to the realisation that you would have a lower chance of an accident happening to you in a brightly coloured car, as compared to a colour that would blend in with its environment. Essentially, wear hi-vis on the road isn't just for looks, it probably helps you stay out of accidents better.

I was impressed with the bus driver. He didn't seem to have trouble tracking the road at any point in the trip, at least I didn't feel it in the steering wheel. Not too surprising, as he has likely driven this route for a decade at this point. He had likely seen way worse road conditions in the past, much before my time.

I did notice that he was driving slower than normal, maybe fifteen kp/h less on average. Its a gentle reminder that even with the massive amount of driving experience that he has, it's never a bad idea to slow down. As the saying goes, its better to arrive late than never.

Imagining myself behind the wheel, I would have been much more nervous. Especially in some of the more fog dense areas, I would probably slow down to half the speed limit.

Would this bother other drivers? Rationally, I know it wouldn't. In those few years, I have never witnessed fog that thick on that route. Obviously other drivers understand why I would be driving at the speed that I was. But there is this irrational nagging feeling in my mind that I'm holding up traffic. I guess that originated from my habit of not getting in other people's way at all cost, instilled into myself when I was younger.

Self driving cars

Current self driving car technology would likely refuse to activate in the fog I saw today. Its not that they cannot work for a technical reason, rather it is these company's working on autonomous system don't want to take liability for when an accident does happen.

Why should I trust the autonomous system when even the makers don't trust it themselves?

I guess you could argue in terms of statistics. Autonomous vehicles will never become sleepy or be under the influence of whatever so that means that they are roughly x times more safer as compared to humans. Today, this is the argument that companies such as Tesla or Waymo are employing.

The flaw with this argument is people tend to value stories over pure numbers. After watching a news segment on TV about a incident relating to self-driving cars, they will immediately take the stance of never trusting these vehicles again. Despite the numbers, people will still argue that these autonomous vehicles will never be safer than humans.

You can't blame these people to much though. Historically, humans have be using stories as a medium to communicate complex ideas, beliefs and ideologies. We have been programmed to place a higher priority on stories above all else, such as statistics.

Testing

In order to improve on the technology behind autonomous vehicles and reduce the number of mistakes they make, companies need testers to test out the system in the real world in real driving situations.

Being a beta tester of these autonomous systems means saving lives. Assuming that the technology will get to a point where the chance of an accident is measured to the eighth decimal, fatality rates will be measure in the triple digits rather than six figures it is today when it is mature enough.

It will get to that point. Machine learning, the largest factor which determines a autonomous vehicles ability, is decreasing its amount of mistakes at an exponential rate. Think about the time gap between the release of ChatGPT and GPT-4. There was improvements across all standardised tests, up to 20%. Rarely do technologies improve exponentially, which make it all the more exciting and probable that computers will learn how to drive on our roads with a high degree of accuracy.

Summary

Your everyday person will never trust an autonomous taxi until it has proven its worth, and its difficult to make a robot worth more than the worst case scenario, especially when human lives are involved.
